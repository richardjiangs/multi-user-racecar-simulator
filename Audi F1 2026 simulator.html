1. The racing line

Outside–inside–outside. Enter from the far edge, cut across the yellow apex dot, release the car to the exit kerb. This maximises the corner radius, so your minimum speed — and everything that follows it — is higher. In Learning Mode the blue line is painted on the road and the zoom map (Circuit tab) shows the exact route ahead.

2. Apex types — and when each is right

There is no single correct apex. Straight after the corner? LATE apex — turn in a beat later, clip past the middle, get on power early ("slow in, fast out"). Hairpin/U-turn onto a straight? Same idea, exaggerated: a V-line with a very late apex. Double-apex same-direction sweeps? An EARLY first apex flows better. S-complex (left-right)? A MID apex that sacrifices this corner to be on line for the next. The boards in Learning Mode read the corner geometry and tell you which one applies — this is the same logic real coaches teach.

3. Racecraft — the line changes in real time

The perfect line is only perfect in clear air. Defending (a car within ~1.5 s behind): brake a touch earlier, take an EARLY apex and own the inside — a slower line that can't be dived on. Attacking (within ~1 s of the car ahead): take a LATER apex for a stronger exit run into the next straight. Learning Mode watches the gaps live and switches the apex advice the moment your race situation changes.

4. Braking — the countdown boards

The red boards count 300 · 200 · 150 · 100 · 50 metres to the apex. Hit maximum brake pressure early in a straight line, then bleed off pressure as you add steering (trail braking) — the released weight keeps the nose planted and the front tyres biting to the TURN IN board.

5. Vision

Look through the corner at the exit, not at the tarmac ahead of the nose. Eyes up to the next reference — the car follows your eyes, and every correction gets smaller.

6. Downforce — grip that grows with speed

An F1 car corners harder the faster it goes: at 250 km/h the wings press it into the road with more than its own weight. Trust the car in fast corners; in slow ones the wings can't help — it's all mechanical grip and traction.

7. 2026 active aero — X-mode & Z-mode

Hold X on the straights: the wings flatten (X-mode) and shed drag. Brake or load the car and it snaps back to high-downforce Z-mode automatically. It replaced DRS in 2026 and works every lap.

8. ERS & the Manual Override (MOM)

Hold V for full electric deploy past the 290 km/h taper. In a race it only arms within ~1 s of the car ahead — the 2026 overtaking rule. The store drains in seconds and recharges under braking.

9. Strat, brake bias, and the pit

Strat Push buys ~2% power at double PU wear; Lean saves fuel and engine. Rearward brake bias rotates the car into slow corners. When the wall calls "box box": drive LEFT into the pit lane in the last 260 m — the limiter is automatic — and stop at YOUR team's garage. The garages run in 2025 championship order from the pit entry (McLaren first, Cadillac last), so driving past other teams' boxes to reach yours is exactly how real F1 works. The crew fixes everything the X-ray shows; refuelling has been banned since 2010.

10. The rulebook that bites

Track limits: all four wheels over the white line deletes the lap; three race warnings is a penalty. Contact damages both cars. The safety car bunches the field — never pass it. Reliability is probabilistic per team: nurse the PU with short-shifts when the engineer warns you.

11. This team: Audi in 2026

The new works PU is recovery-first: the fastest recharge on the grid (lift-and-coast pays double) but modest boost, and reliability plus race starts have hurt (DNS risk is real). Crew 2.9 s; garage 8th from entry.
