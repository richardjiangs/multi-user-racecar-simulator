About this course

Race Car Simulator 101 is for drivers who want to stop guessing and start building laps deliberately. The simulator is fast enough that small mistakes matter: braking too late overloads the front tires, early throttle pushes the car wide, and a careless overtake can ruin two races at once.

The goal is not to memorize one perfect lap. The goal is to understand what the car needs at each phase of a corner so you can adapt to different cars, tracks, traffic, and online race pressure.

Learning objectives

  • Build a lap from reference points: braking marker, turn-in, apex, exit, and recovery point.
  • Choose early, geometric, or late apexes based on the corner that follows.
  • Use trail braking and throttle release to rotate the car without sliding it.
  • Identify safe overtaking zones, set up a pass before the braking zone, and leave racing room.
  • Defend without weaving, blocking late, or destroying your own exit speed.
  • Plan pit timing for online races that use agreed mandatory stops or endurance rules.
  • Drive the simulator's tracks with specific race-line notes instead of treating every corner the same.

Before you begin

You only need one car, one track, and enough patience to repeat the same corner several times. Start in private practice, choose a medium-speed circuit such as Silverstone or Suzuka, and drive at 70 percent until you can complete clean laps without correction.

Driver mindset: speed is the result, not the input. Your inputs are brake pressure, steering angle, throttle timing, and where you place the car.

Curriculum

1

Build the lap map

Mark braking, turn-in, apex, exit, and the next straight. A corner is never isolated; it is a setup for the next piece of track.

2

Brake like a driver

Brake hard while straight, then release pressure as steering increases. The release is what lets the nose bite.

3

Pick the right apex

Use late apexes for slow exits and long straights, geometric apexes for balanced sweepers, and early apexes only when the next corner demands track position.

4

Control throttle and rotation

Throttle should return as the steering unwinds. If you add power before the car is pointed, you create understeer or a slide.

5

Race starts and first laps

Launch cleanly, hold your lane, brake early in traffic, and survive lap one before attacking.

6

Overtake with a plan

Pressure, force a compromised exit, draw alongside before braking, and finish the pass without relying on contact.

7

Defend cleanly

Choose the inside early, make one move, brake in control, and leave room when the other car has overlap.

8

Use pit strategy

For online rooms with mandatory stops, decide whether to undercut, overcut, or stay out based on traffic and pace.

9

Learn track personality

Silverstone rewards flow, Suzuka rewards rhythm, Monaco rewards restraint, Nardo rewards tiny inputs, and the Nurburgring rewards memory.

10

Practice with purpose

Run focused stints: five laps for braking, five laps for exits, five laps in traffic, then compare consistency rather than one lucky lap.

Driving foundation

The corner has four phases

  1. Approach: place the car at the edge that opens the corner. Look past the apex to the exit, not at the front bumper.
  2. Braking: apply the strongest pressure while the car is straight. If the car refuses to turn, you are still asking the front tires to brake too much.
  3. Rotation: release brake pressure as you turn. This keeps weight on the front tires but frees them to steer.
  4. Exit: wait until the car points toward the exit before full throttle. The fastest exit is usually the calmest one.

Apex choice

Apex typeUse it whenDriver habit
Late apexThe corner leads to a long straight or another acceleration zone.Brake a little earlier, turn later, rotate more, and get the car straight sooner.
Geometric apexThe corner is a balanced medium-speed bend with no big exit priority.Use a smooth arc and avoid sawing at the steering.
Early apexYou must position the car for a following corner, chicane, or narrow street section.Accept a slower exit so the next corner becomes easier.
Double apexThe corner is long, such as Suzuka Spoon or wide high-speed bends.Touch inside once, let the car breathe mid-corner, then return to the second apex for exit.

Simple rule: slow corner before long straight equals late apex. Fast flowing sequence equals rhythm and patience. Narrow street corner equals survival first.

Racecraft

Race starts

At the lights, your first job is traction, not hero speed. Launch straight, keep the steering tiny until the car is settled, and expect everyone ahead to brake earlier than they did in practice. In online races, leave more space than you think on lap one because small lag and cold-driver mistakes create accordion braking.

When to overtake

  • Best time: after you get a better exit and are already closing before the braking zone.
  • Safe zones: heavy braking corners after long straights, slow hairpins, and chicanes with clear inside overlap.
  • Bad zones: high-speed esses, blind crests, Monaco Swimming Pool, Suzuka 130R, and the narrow Nordschleife rhythm sections.
  • Pressure pass: follow closely for two corners, force the car ahead to defend, then take the faster exit while they are compromised.

How to overtake

  1. Plan one corner early. If you only decide at the braking marker, it is probably a dive.
  2. Show the car early enough that the other driver can see you.
  3. Brake on the inside in a straight line. Do not slide past the apex and use the other car as your brake.
  4. If your front axle is significantly alongside before turn-in, both cars should leave racing room.
  5. Finish the pass by making the exit, not by pushing the other car off the road.

Defending

Defend before the braking zone, not inside it. Move once, cover the inside, and then drive the corner. If you defend too hard, you ruin your exit and the attacker crosses back under you. A clean defense is early, predictable, and still fast enough to matter.

Traffic and lapping

When approaching slower traffic, pass where the track is straight and the other driver can hold a predictable line. If you are being lapped, do not suddenly brake or jump off-line. Stay predictable, lift slightly on a straight, and let the faster car choose the side.

Track guide

These notes match the simulator's route choices and teach where to place the car. Use them as a first racing line, then refine your braking points based on your car and speed.

Silverstone Circuit fast flow, wide racing surface

  • Abbey and Farm: use a smooth geometric line. Do not over-brake; a lift or brush can be enough when the car is settled.
  • Village and Loop: choose a late apex. The corner is slow and the exit matters more than entry speed.
  • Copse: commit only when the car is straight and stable. Clip the inside once and let the car run wide on exit.
  • Maggotts, Becketts, Chapel: sacrifice the first direction change so the final Chapel exit is open onto Hangar Straight.
  • Stowe: late apex. Overtakes work here if you are alongside before braking; otherwise prepare a better exit for Vale.
  • Vale and Club: heavy braking into Vale is a real passing zone. Do not overshoot; the Club exit decides the next straight.

Suzuka Circuit rhythm and patience

  • Turn 1 and Turn 2: treat Turn 1 as the entry and Turn 2 as the corner that matters. Brake straight, turn in cleanly, and late-apex Turn 2.
  • Esses: no forced overtakes. Each apex prepares the next one; if you attack one too hard, the whole sequence collapses.
  • Degner 1 and 2: Degner 1 is a fast clip, Degner 2 is the exit corner. Prioritize the second apex.
  • Hairpin: strong overtaking spot. Show inside early, brake straight, rotate late, and avoid pushing wide on exit.
  • Spoon: double apex. Be patient between the two apexes and wait for the car to face the back straight before full throttle.
  • 130R and Casio Triangle: 130R is not a dive zone. Set up the pass into Casio, where braking is heavy and the line is clearer.

Circuit de Monaco restraint, exits, and walls

  • Sainte Devote: brake straight and late-apex. The uphill exit is more important than entering wide open.
  • Casino to Mirabeau: use controlled speed and avoid side-by-side racing. Track position matters more than bravery.
  • Grand Hotel Hairpin: very slow, very late apex. Wait, rotate, then unwind. Do not rush throttle.
  • Portier: late apex for tunnel speed. A poor Portier exit ruins the best passing chance at Nouvelle Chicane.
  • Nouvelle Chicane: main overtaking zone after the tunnel. Be alongside before braking and leave room through both parts.
  • Swimming Pool and Rascasse: single-file discipline. Touch the line, avoid panic corrections, and prepare the final corner exit.

Nardo Ring speed discipline

  • Main line: use tiny steering inputs. At high speed, a small correction becomes a big weight transfer.
  • Apex idea: think of the whole ring as one long corner. Hold a stable radius rather than stabbing at the inside.
  • Overtaking: pass with a clear speed difference. Avoid drifting across another car's nose at top speed.
  • Practice goal: learn how each hypercar behaves near top speed and how long it takes to slow down from extreme pace.

Nurburgring GP braking zones and exits

  • Turn 1: heavy braking and late apex. This is a strong passing zone, but only if you can still make the inside curb.
  • Mercedes Arena: slow sequence. Sacrifice the first part for a clean exit from the last part.
  • Schumacher S: high-speed confidence corner. Do not force a pass unless the other car makes a clear mistake.
  • Chicane: overtaking is possible under braking. Keep the car straight over the first part and do not cut back across too early.
  • Final corner: late apex to maximize the start-finish straight. A tidy exit beats a dramatic entry.

Nordschleife memory, survival, and respect

  • Hatzenbach: rhythm first. Avoid overtaking unless the other car clearly gives room.
  • Flugplatz and Schwedenkreuz: settle the car before crests and fast bends. Lift early if the car feels light.
  • Aremberg to Fuchsrohre: late apex Aremberg for the downhill run; keep inputs smooth through compression.
  • Adenauer Forst: brake earlier than pride wants. Many laps die from arriving too fast here.
  • Karussell: slow entry, patient middle, clean exit. Do not fight the steering while the car is loaded.
  • Pflanzgarten: no side-by-side hero moves. Give the car time to land and settle.
  • Dottinger Hohe: safest slipstream passing zone. Plan the pass before Tiergarten, then brake in a straight line.

Pit strategy

The simulator includes a pit limiter, but it does not currently model a complete tire, fuel, and repair pit-stop system. For online races, treat pit stops as agreed race rules: for example, every driver must slow with the pit limiter through a chosen pit window, or complete one reset/pit-lane visit between two lap numbers.

When to pit

  • Undercut: pit earlier when you are stuck in traffic and have clear space behind. The goal is clean laps while the other driver loses time defending.
  • Overcut: stay out when your pace is strong and the car ahead may rejoin into traffic. This works best when you can lap consistently without mistakes.
  • Safety stop: pit after damage, repeated spins, or a mental reset moment. In long online races, a calm reset can save more time than one desperate lap.
  • Monaco rule: track position is king. Do not pit into traffic unless the race rule forces it.
  • Nordschleife rule: avoid pitting from panic. Finish the sector safely first, then decide on the straight.

How to set pit rules for friends

Before the host starts the race, agree on the pit window, lap count, and penalty. Keep it simple: one mandatory pit-limiter pass between laps 2 and 5, no pitting on the final lap, and a 10-second hold if someone forgets.

Practice plan

  1. Five clean laps: no overtakes, no experiments, just finish each lap without leaving the track.
  2. Brake marker drill: pick one braking point and move it five meters later only after three clean attempts.
  3. Apex drill: run the same corner with early, middle, and late apexes. Watch which one gives the best exit speed.
  4. Traffic drill: follow a rival for a lap without passing. Learn where you are faster before attacking.
  5. Race stint: run ten laps and judge consistency. A driver who is 0.5 seconds slower but never crashes often wins online.
