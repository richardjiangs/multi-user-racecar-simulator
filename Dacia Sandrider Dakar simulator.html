1. The roadbook — you navigate, not follow a track

The Dakar has no circuit. Each stage is a point-to-point special across open desert, and the crew navigates from a digital roadbook: distance to the next instruction, a heading (CAP) to hold, and a symbol for the hazard. The 2026 route is an 8,000 km loop from Yanbu on the Red Sea through AlUla, Ha'il, Riyadh, Wadi Ad Dawasir and Bisha, over thirteen stages plus a prologue. In the sim the Circuit tab loads each real stage with its true region, length and terrain; the map shows the piste ahead.

2. Reading the terrain

Saudi Arabia throws every surface at you, and each has its own grip and speed. Hardpack / gravel piste is fast and firm. Camel grass is clumps of vegetation on hummocks that pitch the car. Fesh-fesh is talcum-fine dust hiding holes. Rock and canyon punishes tyres and rims. Soft sand and dunes rob traction and swallow momentum. The sim gives every stage a real surface: the Prologue is firm coastal hardpack (grip ~0.82); the Wadi Ad Dawasir Queen stage is big dunes and fesh-fesh (grip ~0.60). Feel it in the wheel and the speed you can carry.

3. Dune driving — cresting blind

A dune has a windward slope you climb and a slip-face that can drop away vertically on the far side. You never see what's beyond the crest, so you crest slowly and square, ready to lift — land nose-first on the down-slope, not flat off the top. Get it wrong and you endo. In the sim, big dunes tilt the horizon as you climb, and a sharp ridge at speed will launch the car (watch the AIRBORNE flag) — while you're in the air there is no grip, so straighten up before you land.

4. Suspension & 350 mm of travel

A T1+ car runs about 350 mm of wheel travel on long-stroke bypass dampers — that soaks up the washboard and the landings that would destroy a road car. But travel isn't infinite: hammer the rough flat-out and you bottom out, the wheels go light over crests and the grip drops. The sim models the body floating on its suspension (the camera heaves) and cuts grip when the wheels unload — smoothness keeps the tyres on the ground and the car pointing straight.

5. Sand Mode & momentum

In soft sand, wheelspin digs you in — the car bogs and stops. The trick is momentum: pick a gear, keep the revs up and the throttle smooth, and float across the surface rather than clawing at it. Hold X for Sand Mode — it softens the throttle map and helps the car float over soft ground, cutting the risk of bogging. If you do stop in a dune, you're digging boards out by hand.

6. Tyre pressure & punctures

Crews drop tyre pressure for grip and float in the dunes, and pump it back up for the fast rock where a soft tyre gets shredded. It's a constant gamble: run too low on rock and you cut a sidewall; too high in sand and you sink. A puncture out on the stage means changing a 37-inch wheel in the heat, alone. In the sim, rough rock and worn tyres raise the puncture risk (your co-driver warns you), and a failed tyre limps you to assistance.

7. The co-driver & pace notes

The person beside you is reading the roadbook aloud, second by second — "caution, camel grass, 200… danger danger, dune drop… fast right into soft." A good co-driver is half your speed. In the sim, press H or the Co-Driver button for spoken calls; the live roadbook reads the terrain and the corner ahead, and warns you before punctures, bogging and assistance.

8. Marathon stages & mechanical sympathy

2026 has two marathon stages (Stages 4-5 and 9-10): at the overnight bivouac the crews get no outside assistance — whatever breaks, you and your co-driver fix it, or start the next stage broken. That makes mechanical sympathy everything: nurse the engine, don't smash every rock, save the car for the finish. In Rally Stage Mode the car accumulates real stress — engine, gearbox, driveshafts, brakes, tyres, suspension — and a system pushed to its limit fails for good.

9. Assistance — refuel & repairs

Unlike F1, Dakar cars refuel, and outside the marathon a service crew waits at the bivouac. In Rally Stage Mode, drive off the piste into the assistance area in the last stretch of the loop: the crew fills the tank, changes tyres and fixes what the X-ray shows — each repair costs time, and you're released to rejoin. Manage when you stop; every minute stationary is a minute lost to the clock.

10. Waypoints, speed zones & penalties

You must pass hidden GPS waypoints to prove you drove the real route — miss one and you take a time penalty. Villages and sensitive areas are speed zones (often 30-90 km/h) enforced to the second by GPS; a few km/h over is minutes of penalty. Straying wildly off the roadbook line loses time and risks getting lost. Dakar is won on the total time of every stage added up — consistency beats heroics.

11. This car: Dacia Sandrider, 2026

The Sandrider is a Prodrive-built T1+ Ultimate car with a mid-mounted 3.0 twin-turbo V6 from the Nissan Z, restricted to ~360 hp, on a tubular chassis under a low-drag carbon body — Dacia claims ~10% less drag and 40% less lift than its rivals. Driven by Sébastien Loeb, Nasser Al-Attiyah and Cristina Gutiérrez, it won the 2026 Dakar at the second attempt. Low, slippery and front-light — quick across the top of the dunes.

Learning Mode paints the terrain read-out and the fast line across the stage, marks the crests and the corners, and shows the piste ahead on a zoomed map inset — plus the live co-driver roadbook.
